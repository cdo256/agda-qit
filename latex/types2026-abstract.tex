\section{Introduction}

Quotient inductive types (QITs) were introduced as a set-level
fragment of higher inductive types suitable for internalising syntax
in type theory~\cite{altenkirch2016-qit}. In a UIP or set-truncated
setting, the higher structure collapses, but the idea of specifying
data together with identifications remains, leading to quotient
inductive types with initial-algebra semantics and elimination by
initiality rather than by quotienting raw syntax
afterwards~\cite{altenkirch2018-qiit,kaposi2019-constructing,kovacs2020-large}. This
talk focuses on a basic obstruction in that picture: although a QIT
may at first seem to be just an ordinary inductive type quotiented by
equations, extensional quotienting forgets information that
constructors may still need. This tension becomes especially visible
in the infinitary case, where known general constructions appeal to
weak choice principles. Using a simple running example, I explain why
this happens and why weak quotients and setoid-style presentations
suggest a different constructive moral.

\section{Constructing QITs}

A quotient inductive type is not
merely a quotient of raw terms, but an initial algebra for a signature
with equations. If $F_\Sigma(X)$ is the polynomial functor associated
to a signature $\Sigma = (S,P)$, then a candidate quotient inductive
type is given by an algebra
\[
\mathsf{sup} : F_\Sigma(Q) \to Q
\]
together with the expected universal property. The point of this
presentation is that it packages the constructors and the eliminator at
the same time: what matters is not only which terms are identified, but
also how the resulting object supports recursion and induction. This is
the perspective developed in the semantic treatment of QIITs by
Altenkirch, Capriotti, Dijkstra, Kraus and Nordvall
Forsberg~\cite{altenkirch2018-qiit}, and later extended to finitary and
large/infinitary signature languages by Kaposi, Kov\'acs and
collaborators~\cite{kaposi2019-constructing,kovacs2020-large}.

\section{The lifting problem}

The central example of the talk is the type of \emph{mobiles}: countably
branching trees in which permuting the children does not change the
resulting element. Writing $M$ for the desired quotient type, one may
present it schematically by constructors
\[
\ell : M,
\qquad
n : (\mathbb{N}\to M)\to M,
\qquad
\mathsf{perm}_\pi(f) : n(f\circ \pi)=n(f)
\]
for each permutation $\pi : \mathrm{Perm}(\mathbb{N})$.

The naive construction starts from the raw $W$-type $W$ of ordered
countably branching trees and forms a quotient $W/{\sim}$ by the
permutation equations. At first glance this looks exactly right. But the
problem appears as soon as we ask for the constructor on the quotient.
The leaf constructor lifts immediately by sending $\ell_W$ to its
equivalence class. For the node constructor, however, we would need to
define
\[
n_{\sim} : (W/\sim)^{\mathbb{N}} \to W/\sim.
\]
The obvious route is: given a family of quotient elements, choose a raw
tree representing each one, apply the raw constructor in $W$, and then
quotient again. But this requires choosing representatives.

This is the basic lifting problem:

\begin{quote}
a quotient gives equivalence classes, but a constructor wants
representatives.
\end{quote}

That is the conceptual centre of the talk. It explains why quotient
classes alone are not enough: a QIT must still support elimination, and
that means the constructors must be well defined on quotient elements,
not just on chosen representatives.

\section{Why the problem is choice-shaped}

This obstruction already has the shape of a choice principle. If the
quotient map $[-]_\sim : W \twoheadrightarrow W/\sim$ admitted a section, then
we could pick a representative tree for each quotient element and define
$n_M$ by lifting along that section. In $\mathrm{Set}$, the
principle that every surjection splits is equivalent to the axiom of
choice. Thus the difficulty is not accidental: choice appears precisely
when extensional quotienting has forgotten data that the constructor
still needs.

In the infinitary case this phenomenon is reflected in existing general
constructions. Fiore, Pitts and Steenkamp introduce a broad class of
indexed quotient-inductive types, called QWI types, and show that they
can be constructed from inductive types and quotient types under WISC
(or, equivalently for the relevant argument, IWISC in the form used in
their paper)~\cite{fiore2022-qwit}. Their construction proceeds
stagewise, building size-bounded approximations and then taking a
colimit. The hard step is showing that forming constructor inputs is
compatible with this stagewise colimit; this is exactly where the weak
choice principle enters.

This fits a wider semantic picture. Higher inductive types are designed
to build identifications into the type former itself rather than by
external quotienting, and Lumsdaine and Shulman give general semantics
for such constructions in suitable model categories~\cite{lumsdaine2020-hit}.
From the present viewpoint, the contrast is not that one setting has
problems and the other does not, but that different equality formalisms
forget different amounts of information.

\section{Choosing not to forget}

The second half of the talk takes this as a design hint. If choice
appears because extensional quotients forget too much, then perhaps a
more constructive notion of quotient should forget less. This motivates
weak quotients and setoid-style presentations, where points are retained
and equality is carried as explicit structure rather than collapsed
immediately to bare equivalence classes. On this view, one does not need
to recover representatives later, because the witness data was never
thrown away in the first place.

This does \emph{not} mean that weak quotients solve everything. They
solve one problem by introducing another. In a setoid or displayed-setoid
semantics, a dependent family over a context must not only assign fibres
to points, but also provide transport maps along equality witnesses,
together with the required coherence conditions. So the difficulty moves
from choosing representatives to managing coherent transport. Kaposi and
Xie's work on quotient inductive-inductive types in the setoid model is
a useful reference point here: it shows how much can be recovered in a
strict setoid semantics, but also makes clear that plain setoids do not
automatically provide all the coherence one would like for dependent
elimination~\cite{kaposi2021-setoid}.

\section{Conclusion}

The aim of the talk is not to present a completed new construction,
but to sharpen the conceptual picture. Using mobiles as a simple
infinitary example, I argue that quotient inductive types are
difficult because extensional quotients erase witness data and then
require something choice-like when that data must later be
recovered. The stagewise construction of Fiore, Pitts and Steenkamp
shows how far this can be repaired using weak choice, while weak
quotients suggest a different constructive moral: do not forget the
witness data too early. The price, however, is that dependent type
theory must then carry coherent transport explicitly. In this sense,
the real issue is not whether we quotient, but what information our
notion of equality is allowed to forget.
