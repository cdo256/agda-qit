\section{Introduction and Motivation}

Quotient Inductive Types (QITs) are a central mechanism for specifying
datatypes equipped with identifications in set-truncated Homotopy Type
Theory \cite{hott2013-book}. They support a wide range of constructions,
including the HoTT reals \cite{hott2013-book}, partiality monads
\cite{altenkirch2017-partiality-monad}, and the syntax of type theory
\cite{altenkirch2018-qiit}.

Despite their expressive power, QITs present significant foundational
difficulties. Lumsdaine and Shulman showed that certain quotient
constructions, including Brower's definition of the countable
ordinals\cite{kraus2023-ordinal},
cannot in general be obtained using quotients alone
\cite{lumsdaine2020-hit}. This suggests that
some form of infinitary principle or choice may be unavoidable in a
constructive metatheory.

Fiore, Pitts, and Steenkamp subsequently showed that a broad class of
QITs, which they call quotient-W types (QW types), admit an initial
algebra semantics assuming the Weak Initial Set of Covers (WISC)
principle \cite{fiore2022-quotients-inductive-types}. Their result
recovers many important examples, including extensional countable
ordinals. However, WISC remains a nontrivial choice principle
\cite{vandenberg2012-wisc-axiom}, and its necessity is poorly
understood.

This leads to the central question of this work:

\begin{quote}
Which quotient-W types can be constructed fully constructively, without
assuming any form of choice?
\end{quote}

We propose \emph{bounded locality} as a criterion separating
constructively obtainable QW types from genuinely non-constructive ones.
Intuitively, locality measures how deeply one must inspect the
inductive structure of terms in order to witness an equation. In
pathological examples, such as extensional ordinals, equality may
require unbounded descent into subtrees. By contrast, in examples such
as infinite mobiles, equality preserves depth and only inspects
structure within a bounded range. We argue that this boundedness is
exactly what allows the key step of the construction to go through
without choice.

\section{Background}

We briefly recall the framework of quotient-W types introduced by Fiore,
Pitts, and Steenkamp \cite{fiore2022-quotients-inductive-types}. A QW
signature consists of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item a container \(\Sigma = S \triangleleft P\)
          \cite{abbott2005-containers}, specifying the underlying
          polynomial functor, and
    \item a system of equations \((E,V,l,r)\), where each equation
          \(e : E\) has a set of variables \(V(e)\) and left- and
          right-hand sides in the free \(\Sigma\)-algebra over \(V(e)\).
\end{itemize}

From such a signature, Fiore et al.~construct a diagram of
approximations indexed by a size structure. Intuitively, this diagram
represents successive stages of the quotient construction, where each
stage gives the equalities visible within a bounded depth. The desired
QW type is then obtained as the colimit \(Q\) of this diagram.

To show that this colimit is an initial algebra, one must establish in
particular the \emph{cocontinuity} of the associated polynomial functor:
forming a constructor after quotienting must agree with quotienting
after forming a constructor. In the work of Fiore et al., WISC is used
at this step to size-bound families of subtrees and construct inverse
maps witnessing the required isomorphism.

Our work revisits this step. We observe that the need for WISC arises
precisely when equations allow arbitrarily deep inspection of terms.
When all equations are depth-bounded, the required coherence data can
instead be constructed by well-founded recursion on a fixed ordinal
bound \(\alpha\), avoiding any appeal to choice.

\section{Mobiles}

Our motivating example is the quotient inductive type of
\emph{mobiles}: well-founded trees quotiented by permutation at each
node. This example is simple enough to analyse directly, while still
being representative of the infinitary behaviour relevant to QW types.

Specifically, for an index set \(I\), one considers a type generated by
a leaf constructor, a node constructor with \(I\)-indexed branches, and
a path constructor identifying nodes whose branches differ only by a
permutation of \(I\).

\section{Locality and Main Result}

We formalise locality by assigning a rank function
\[
\mathrm{rank} : T_\Sigma X \to \mathcal{O}
\]
measuring the height of the underlying tree. An equation system is said
to be \(\alpha\)-local if, for every equation \(e : E\), both sides
\(l(e)\) and \(r(e)\) have rank at most a fixed ordinal \(\alpha\).
Intuitively, this means that every generating equation only inspects
structure up to depth \(\le \alpha\).

Our main result is that \(\alpha\)-local QW signatures admit fully
constructive initial algebra semantics.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Theorem.}
\emph{Let \(\Sigma\) be a QW signature whose equations are
\(\alpha\)-local. Then the associated quotient-W type exists as an
initial algebra, and its construction does not require WISC or any
other choice principle.}
\medskip

The proof follows the outline of Fiore--Pitts--Steenkamp
\cite{fiore2022-quotients-inductive-types}, but replaces the use of
WISC in the cocontinuity argument by recursion on the bound
\(\alpha\). This yields the necessary coherence data constructively.

\section{Discussion}

This analysis explains why certain examples genuinely require choice.
For extensional ordinals, equality is defined by mutual containment of
downward closures, which requires unbounded descent into subtrees
\cite{fiore2022-quotients-inductive-types}. No fixed ordinal bound
\(\alpha\) suffices, so the quotient is non-local. This accounts for the
essential use of WISC in that case.

By contrast, boundedly local quotients, such as mobiles, admit a fully
constructive treatment. More generally, our results suggest that the
need for choice in quotient constructions is controlled not by
infinitary branching alone, but by whether the equations require
unbounded inspection of substructure.
