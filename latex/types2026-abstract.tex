\section{Introduction and Motivation}

Quotient Inductive Types (QITs) are a central mechanism for specifying
datatypes equipped with identifications in set-truncated Homotopy Type
Theory \cite{hott2013-book}. They support a wide range of constructions,
including the HoTT reals \cite{hott2013-book}, partiality monads
\cite{altenkirch2017-partiality-monad}, and the syntax of type theory
\cite{altenkirch2018-qiit}.

Despite their expressive power, QITs present significant foundational
difficulties. Lumsdaine and Shulman showed that certain quotient
constructions, including Brouwer's definition of the countable ordinals
\cite{kraus2023-ordinal}, cannot in general be obtained using quotients
alone \cite{lumsdaine2020-hit}. This suggests that some form of
infinitary principle or choice may be unavoidable in a constructive
metatheory.

Fiore, Pitts, and Steenkamp subsequently showed that a broad class of
QITs, which they call quotient-W types (QW types), admit an initial
algebra semantics assuming the Weak Initial Set of Covers (WISC)
principle \cite{fiore2022-quotients-inductive-types}. Their result
recovers many important examples, including extensional countable
ordinals. However, WISC remains a nontrivial choice principle
\cite{vandenberg2012-wisc-axiom}, and its necessity is poorly
understood.

This leads to the question we study in this talk: can one isolate a
simple infinitary example in which the use of weak choice can be
separated from the quotient construction itself? Our motivating example
is the quotient inductive type of \emph{mobiles}, that is, countably
branching trees quotiented by permutation of children. We argue that
mobiles form a useful test case because their equations are infinitary
but syntactically simple, and in particular preserve depth.

\section{Background}

We briefly recall the framework of quotient-W types introduced by Fiore,
Pitts, and Steenkamp \cite{fiore2022-quotients-inductive-types}. A QW
signature consists of a container \(\Sigma = S \triangleleft P\)
\cite{abbott2005-containers} together with a system of equations
\((E, V, l, r)\), where each equation \(e : E\) has a set of variables
\(V(e)\) and left- and right-hand sides in the free
\(\Sigma\)-algebra over \(V(e)\).

From such a signature, Fiore et al.\ construct a diagram of
depth-bounded approximations \(D_\alpha\) indexed by a size structure,
and obtain the desired quotient inductive type as the colimit \(Q\). To
show that this colimit is an initial algebra, one must establish the
\emph{cocontinuity} of the associated polynomial functor: forming a
constructor after quotienting must agree with quotienting after forming a
constructor. In the work of Fiore et al., WISC is used at this step to
size-bound families of subtrees and construct the required inverse maps.

\section{Mobiles as a test case}

The QIT of mobiles \(\mathcal{M}\) is generated by a leaf constructor
\(\ell : \mathcal{M}\), a node constructor
\(n : (\mathbb{N} \to \mathcal{M}) \to \mathcal{M}\),
and a path constructor identifying \(n(f \circ \pi) = n(f)\) for each
permutation \(\pi : \mathbb{N} \cong \mathbb{N}\).

The naive quotient \(Q := W/{\sim}\) of the raw \(\mathbb{N}\)-branching
tree type \(W\) by permutation has the right extensional shape, but the
difficulty is to equip it with the constructor structure of the intended
QIT\@. To define the node constructor on \(Q\), one would need to lift a
family of quotient elements to representatives in \(W\), which is
precisely a countably infinite choice problem.

For mobiles, the defining equations \emph{preserve depth}: permuting the
children of a node does not change their depths or the depth of the
resulting tree. This means that equal elements of \(Q\) lie in the same
approximation stage, which suggests that stage assignment should respect
the quotient and that the choice step in the FPS argument may be
avoidable in this case.

\section{Relative construction from extensional plump ordinals}

We assume an \emph{extensional plump ordinal basis}
\cite{taylor1996-plump}: an ordinal object \(Z\) equipped with a
sup constructor
\[
  \mathsf{sup} : \textstyle\sum_{s:S}(P_s \to Z) \to Z
\]
for the given signature, together with the usual order structure
\(\le, <\), satisfying the extensionality principle that each ordinal is
determined by its set of strictly smaller ordinals. Extensionality is
what makes the stage object well-behaved under permutation: two ordinals
witnessing mutual upper bounds are literally equal, rather than merely
equivalent.

The key step is that the raw stage assignment
\[
  \mathrm{rank}_0 : W \to Z,
  \qquad
  \mathrm{rank}_0(\mathrm{node}\,f) = \sup_i\,\mathrm{rank}_0(f_i),
\]
respects the permutation quotient at each stage precisely because the
equations are depth-preserving. It therefore descends to maps
\(\mathrm{rank}_\alpha : D_\alpha \to Z\) on each approximation, and
these are compatible with the connecting maps of the diagram. This yields
a coherent stage assignment
\(\mathrm{rank} : \mathrm{colim}\,D \to Z\),
which provides the section needed to define the constructors on the
colimit without appealing to the full cocontinuity proof via WISC\@.

The quotient inductive type of mobiles is therefore obtained from the
stagewise argument relative to an extensional plump ordinal basis. This
argument has been partially formalised in Agda.

\section{Beyond mobiles: locality and barriers}

The depth-preservation property of mobiles is a purely syntactic
condition: one can read it off directly from the equations. A natural
question is whether a weaker condition suffices.

We say that an equation system is \emph{\(\alpha\)-local} if both sides
of every equation have rank at most a fixed ordinal \(\alpha\) in the
free algebra over the variables. Locality is a tempting weakening of
depth preservation, and may capture a broader class of examples for
which a similar ordinal argument could apply.

However, locality is not yet a proved criterion, and bounded syntax alone
does not control ordinal strength in general. Blass showed that even
apparently well-controlled equations can generate large ordinal strength
\cite{blass1983-words}, and Lumsdaine and Shulman later reframed this
barrier in the higher inductive type setting \cite{lumsdaine2020-hit}.
A satisfactory general syntactic condition therefore remains open.

Our working conjecture is that the quotient inductive type of mobiles is
not derivable from UIP + funExt + Prop + quotients + \(W\)-types alone,
without an additional ordinal principle. If true, this would show that
the extensional plump ordinal basis is not merely an artefact of the
proof strategy, but a genuine requirement. Proving or refuting this
conjecture is the main open problem left by this work.
